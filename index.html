Disruption


     To gain a better, more objective understanding of the future of American jobs, we analyzed data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics and displayed our findings in 3 different maps: 1) Projected job growth (opportunity); 2) Projected job loss (disruption); and 3) Magnitude of projected change in an area due to jobs (combined), a factor of opportunity and disruption. Data was provided at the MSA and NMA region level: Metropolitan Statistical Areas and Non-metropolitan Areas, respectively, denoting areas with uniform economic characteristics equivalent to 2/3 cities. Each MSA or NMA was awarded a disruption, opportunity, or combined value based on 20 year job projections and current employment levels in the area.

Interesting Findings from data:

  • The Southeast displayed notably high levels of opportunity and projected job growth, with opportunity values similar to those of more urban, well-known cities in the Northeast
  • Regarding disruption, parts of Central California and the Southeast, especially South Carolina, stood out as areas with especially high values
  • Nationwide, jobs that were manual in nature and easily replacable by far represented the biggest areas for job loss
  • Nationwide, jobs related to the Healthcare industry and personal in nature (such as Physical Therapist Assistants, Home Health Aides) by far represented the largest contributors to job opportunity. This could be a factor of either the immunity of these jobs due to their personal, un-automizable nature; or the increased need for healthcare related to recent legislature.
